Research & Evidence

The scientific foundations and evidence base supporting the IntraAct literacy method.

Scientific Foundation

Grounded in Reading Science

The IntraAct method is built on decades of cognitive science research into how the brain learns to read.

Cognitive Neuroscience

Based on research by Fritz Jansen and Uta Streit into the neural processes underlying reading acquisition and how the brain develops reading pathways.

National Reading Panel

Aligned with the "Big Five" reading components: phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension as identified by the NRP.

Science of Reading

Consistent with the science of reading consensus that explicit, systematic instruction is the most effective approach to literacy development.

Key Findings

What the Research Shows

Explicit Instruction Matters

Research consistently demonstrates that explicit, systematic phonics instruction leads to significantly better reading outcomes than implicit or embedded approaches, especially for struggling readers.

Source: National Reading Panel, 2000

Fluency Builds Comprehension

When word recognition becomes automatic through practice, students can devote cognitive resources to understanding meaning. Guided oral reading with feedback has been shown to improve fluency significantly.

Source: National Reading Panel, 2000

Feedback Accelerates Learning

Immediate corrective feedback during reading practice helps students develop accurate decoding habits and prevents the reinforcement of errors, leading to faster skill acquisition.

Source: Cognitive Science Research

Structured Repetition Works

Spaced and structured repetition strengthens neural pathways for reading, building automaticity in word recognition. This is a core principle of effective literacy instruction.

Source: Learning Science Research
